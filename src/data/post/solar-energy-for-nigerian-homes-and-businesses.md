Nigeria receives some of the highest solar irradiance levels in the world — averaging **5 to 7 peak sun hours** per day across most of the country. Yet millions of homes and businesses continue to rely on expensive, polluting petrol generators. The solar energy revolution is already underway in Nigeria, and those who invest now will reap significant long-term savings.

## Why Solar Makes Sense in Nigeria Right Now

The economics of solar have shifted dramatically in the last decade. Panel costs have fallen by over **90% since 2010**, and battery storage technology has improved considerably. Meanwhile, diesel and petrol prices in Nigeria have risen sharply, making generator operation increasingly costly.

For a typical Nigerian business running a 5kVA generator 10 hours a day:

- **Fuel cost**: ₦2,000–₦3,500 per day (at current pump prices)
- **Maintenance**: oil changes, carburettor servicing, regular repairs
- **Noise and emissions**: impacting staff comfort and air quality

A properly sized solar system eliminates or drastically reduces all of these costs with a typical payback period of **3–5 years**, after which the power is essentially free.

## Understanding a Basic Solar System

A complete solar installation has four key components:

### 1. Solar Panels
The panels convert sunlight to DC electricity. We install monocrystalline panels — the most efficient type — from reputable manufacturers with 25-year performance warranties.

### 2. Inverter
The inverter converts DC power to the AC power your appliances use. For most Nigerian installations, a **hybrid inverter** is the smart choice — it manages solar input, battery charging, and grid or generator backup automatically.

### 3. Battery Bank
Batteries store energy for use at night or on cloudy days. We typically recommend lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO₄) batteries for their long cycle life (3,000+ charge cycles), safety, and consistent performance in Nigeria's heat.

### 4. Charge Controller
This regulates the flow of power from panels to batteries, preventing overcharging. Modern hybrid inverters often include this functionality built in.

## Sizing Your System

System sizing depends on your load (what appliances you want to power) and how many hours you need backup. Common residential configurations:

| System Size | Typical Load Covered |
|---|---|
| 1.5kW | Lights, fans, phone charging, small TV |
| 3kW | Above + fridge, laptop, router |
| 5kW | Full home including AC (1–1.5HP) |
| 10kW+ | Large homes, small businesses |

Our team conducts a **free load assessment** before recommending a system, ensuring you don't over-invest or end up underpowered.

## What About the Grid?

Our installations can be configured to work **with** the grid when it's available, topping up batteries or feeding surplus back. When the grid fails — as it frequently does — the system switches to solar and battery automatically, with no interruption.

## Common Concerns We Hear

**"The panels will get too hot and stop working."**
All panels lose a small amount of efficiency in extreme heat, but they are designed to operate in tropical climates. Performance loss is typically 1–2% at peak heat — negligible.

**"What if it's cloudy for several days?"**
We size battery banks to provide autonomy for 1–3 days of low sun, depending on budget. We can also configure the system to use a generator as a backup charger in extended cloudy periods.

**"Is it worth it for a small home?"**
Even a modest 1.5kW system can eliminate your lighting and small appliance load, saving ₦50,000–₦80,000 annually in generator fuel for a typical Kaduna home.
